Multi-User Risks Prevention

Multi-User misuses can occur when several users do not cooperate toward a common goal, but instead, perform modifications that result in unexpected or unsafe system behavior.

NOTE: Two users, unaware of each other’s actions, could perform conflicting application modifications or deployments. These actions could then result in unexpected application behavior.

The consequences of such misuse can lead to:

  • Unexpected change of application behavior.

  • Unexpected running or interruption of operations.

  • Lack of a user’s awareness of the changes in the Distributed Control Application.

To help prevent such misuses, Multi-User provides the zone feature.

NOTE: To have more information about the zone feature, refer to the Multi-User Zone Feature topic.

In addition, every user must organize their usage of the resources in a cooperative way:

  • By defining a workflow followed by every user.

    NOTE: One recommended workflow is detailed in the Multi-User Workflow chapter.

  • By informing other users of one’s requirements and purposes.

  • By informing other users of one’s interactions with the system.

This way, every users would follow a defined workflow oriented toward a common and shared goal for the Distributed Control Application that other users would be aware of.

Example of Multi-User goals:

  • Modifying the operating state of a device (Run, Stop…).

  • Modifying the application in the device (Online Change, Full Deploy).

  • Modifying the boot project of a device.

Example of Multi-User best practices:

  • Defining, planning and sharing system updates requirements.

  • Defining users’ roles and responsibilities.

  • Granting adequate access right to each user.

  • Organizing work collaboration and communication between users.
